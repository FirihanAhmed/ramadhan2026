The Research Basis

The Mongol advance destroyed Baghdad in 1258, ending five centuries of Abbasid rule and what many historians consider the Islamic Golden Age. The Mongols had already conquered Persia, taken Aleppo and Damascus, and reached Gaza. The Mamluk victory at Ain Jalut on September 3, 1260 , in the Jezreel Valley , was the first permanent halt to Mongol westward expansion.

This network models the documented downstream consequences across six layers: the immediate geographic shield (Egypt, the Hejaz, the Levant), the regional powers preserved (North Africa, the Mamluk state, Arabian trade), the knowledge and trade networks that survived (Islamic scholarship institutions, Silk Road alternatives, Mediterranean commerce), the civilizational systems that depended on them (the translation movement that fed Arabic texts into Latin, university systems, agricultural transfers), and the global downstream (the European Renaissance, the Scientific Revolution, and the modern world's intellectual foundations).

Each connection in this graph reflects a documented historical dependency. The cascade model uses betweenness centrality to identify Ain Jalut's structural role: it sits on the shortest path between the Mongol advance and 82% of downstream nodes. Removing it opens propagation to all layers.
